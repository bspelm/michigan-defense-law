What Felonies Can Be Expunged in Michigan Under the 2023 Clean Slate Law?

The 2023 Clean Slate law changed how people with criminal records can pursue relief in Michigan. If you are wondering what felonies can be expunged in michigan, this article outlines the general categories of convictions the law addresses, the types of cases typically excluded, and practical steps to determine eligibility for record clearance.

Overview of the 2023 Clean Slate Law

The Clean Slate initiative expanded opportunities to set aside or expunge certain convictions, aiming to reduce barriers to employment, housing, and education. Rather than automatically sealing all records, the law establishes eligibility rules tied to the nature of the offense, completion of sentence requirements, and a waiting period without subsequent convictions. The change reflects a broader move toward reintegration while protecting public safety.

Which Felonies Are Potentially Eligible?

Many nonviolent felonies can be considered for expungement under the statute. Common categories that often qualify include:

  • Drug possession offenses that did not involve sale or distribution in a way that triggers additional penalties
  • Property offenses such as smaller-scale theft or receiving stolen property where no significant bodily harm occurred
  • Certain financial crimes that were nonviolent and did not involve large-scale fraud affecting public benefits
  • Some low-level felonies related to controlled substances or paraphernalia that meet statutory criteria

To answer what felonies can be expunged in michigan in any specific situation, it is important to compare the exact statutory language with the conviction record and sentencing details. Case-level facts — like whether the offense was enhanced or whether restitution was ordered — can change eligibility.

Felonies That Are Typically Ineligible

The law maintains exclusions for offenses that pose clear risks or involve severe misconduct. Violent felonies, many sexual offenses, and convictions that require registration as a sex offender are usually off-limits. Homicide, offenses with mandatory life sentences, and certain class 1 felonies with aggravating factors are generally excluded as well. These exclusions reflect a balance between second chances and community protection.

When trying to determine exclusions, focus on the statute’s definitions and any accompanying administrative rules. Those boundaries help clarify what felonies can be expunged in michigan and which convictions will remain part of the public record.

How the Process Works

Applying for relief typically requires demonstrating that the sentence is complete, fines and restitution are addressed, and the individual has remained conviction-free for the statutory waiting period. Depending on the offense and any prior record, courts may require a petition, supporting documents, and sometimes a hearing. In some instances, record sealing may occur automatically; in others, a formal application is necessary.

Eligibility often hinges on precise timelines and conditions; understanding what felonies can be expunged in michigan requires checking those requirements carefully and gathering court records that confirm completion of sentence terms.

Potential Impact and Next Steps

Successful expungement can remove barriers to work, housing, and education by limiting public access to conviction details. However, certain government agencies and specific licensing processes may still consider expunged records in narrow circumstances. If you are evaluating whether your past conviction might be cleared, a careful review will show which categories of crimes qualify and what felonies can be expunged in michigan, allowing you to plan the next legal steps.

Conclusion

The Clean Slate law broadened relief for many individuals with nonviolent felony convictions while preserving exclusions for serious offenses. Reviewing your criminal history against the statute’s criteria and confirming sentence completion are the first steps toward expungement. Taking those steps can help determine whether you qualify and how to proceed with a petition or application for record clearance.

How to Qualify for Felony Expungement in Michigan State Courts

Understanding how to clear a felony from your public criminal record can open doors to employment and housing. If you are researching eligibility, a common question is what felonies can be expunged in Michigan and what steps the courts require. This article explains the core criteria used by Michigan State Courts, outlines typical exclusions, and describes the procedural steps to seek record relief.

Overview of Eligibility Requirements

Michigan law sets specific conditions that must be met before a felony conviction may be considered for expungement. Generally, courts look at the nature of the offense, whether all parts of the sentence have been completed, and if the person has remained free of new convictions for a defined waiting period. Meeting these baseline conditions does not guarantee relief but is essential to move forward with a petition.

Basic Elements Courts Review

When reviewing a petition, judges commonly verify: sentence completion dates, restitution and fines paid, active probation or parole status, and whether any statutory waiting period has expired. The presence of additional criminal history can affect timing and eligibility. Accurate court records and sentencing documents are necessary to prove these elements during the filing process.

Which Felonies May Qualify

Certain nonviolent felonies and lower-level offenses are often the kinds of convictions considered for set-aside or sealing under Michigan rules. To determine what felonies can be expunged in Michigan for your situation, compare the statutory definitions with the exact offense listed on the disposition. Some convictions that were downgraded, involved minimal harm, or were tied to personal substance use may fall into qualifying categories when other conditions are satisfied.

Felonies Typically Excluded

There are clear limits to relief. Convictions that involve serious violence, homicide, many sexual offenses, or those requiring sex-offender registration are usually not eligible for expungement. Additionally, offenses that carry mandatory life sentences or specific statutory exclusions remain part of the public record in most cases. If you are unsure whether a conviction is excluded, review the statute closely to see whether your charge appears on the prohibited list and whether any enhancements or related charges change the classification.

Practical Steps to File

Before filing in Michigan State Courts, gather certified copies of criminal dockets, sentencing documents, and proof of payment for fines or restitution. Typical filings include a formal petition and supporting affidavits that demonstrate compliance with sentence terms and waiting periods. The court may schedule a hearing and provide notice to prosecutors or victims, depending on the rules that apply.

  • Obtain certified record and sentencing paperwork
  • Confirm all financial obligations are satisfied
  • Verify the waiting period has elapsed without new convictions
  • Prepare and file the petition with the appropriate court

What to Expect After Filing

Courts may grant relief, deny the petition, or place conditions on the sealing order. Even when records are sealed, certain government agencies or licensing boards might retain limited access under specified circumstances. If a petition is denied, the court’s decision will explain the reasons, which may include ineligibility based on the offense type or failure to meet statutory requirements.

Further Clarification and Next Steps

If you are preparing to pursue relief, it helps to map the timeline of your sentence and collect documentation that supports compliance. Asking whether what felonies can be expunged in Michigan applies to a particular charge will guide preparation and filing strategy. In complex cases, reviewing the statute in detail and confirming the precise wording of your conviction will reduce surprises during court review.

Conclusion

Securing felony expungement through Michigan State Courts depends on satisfying statutory conditions, assembling the right documents, and following court procedures. By identifying whether your offense qualifies and ensuring all sentence requirements are complete, you can determine what felonies can be expunged in Michigan and take the necessary steps to file a petition for relief.
